Email-Only Sign-In: Revolutionizing User Authentication

Start Reading

Introduction

Hopefully, this white paper is an easy-to-read guide that breaks down the benefits of using just your email address to sign in. It explains how this method can reduce the number of steps for a user to authenticate.

Executive Overview

Email address-only sign-in is a new way to authenticate users. With email address-only sign-in, you can use any identity provider that allows users to sign in with an email address. By removing passwords and credential management from your application, you can improve the usability and security of your application.

You've probably seen the term "passwordless" thrown around lately. But what does it mean? Passwordless authentication is a way of signing into websites that uses your email address instead of the usuual email-address/password combination. If you're like most people, you have more than one account on different websites (e.g., Facebook and LinkedIn). To sign in to these sites, you need to remember your passwords — which can be hard when there are so many websites!

Passwordless authentication is designed to make things easier for users by eliminating the need to remember multiple passwords across all online accounts. The benefit? No more remembering countless passwords! Instead, with passwordless authentication, users only need their email addresses to access any service that supports this feature.

Not only does passwordless authentication simplify life for users but it also provides better security than traditional logins because every time someone logs into an account using their email address instead of a password, we're able to generate an additional layer of protection called two-factor authentication (2FA).

Do we need passwords to sign into websites in 2023?

Even if you don't know it, you've probably used passwordless authentication before. Passwordless authentication is called Single Sign-On (SSO) because it allows you to sign in automatically just once and access all your sites from that point forward. For example, you are signing into your email account or completing a purchase on Amazon without entering a password.

The problem with passwords is that they are easily stolen by hackers, resold online, and used for fraud and identity theft. The standard solution for this has been two-factor authentication (2FA). But even 2FA can be bypassed by hackers with enough skill or money—it's just another layer of defense against bad actors on the internet.

Email address only sign-in - how it works!

When you enter your email address (only) in the box to sign in and click submit, various pieces of information about you (e.g., session, your location, typical time of day you sign-in, etc.) and your machine are saved for later use to basically say this is the person and machine who is trying to sign in.

After hitting submit, you are usually prompted to check your email. When you check your email, you will find a link to click. This link contains at its fundamental level a key with information about the person/machine who clicked the button to sign-in. When you click the link from your email, if all criteria matches, you are logged in and have been identified and authenticated.

Ideally the only one who has access to your email inbox is you! Still, forwarding the email, or even if your email got intercepted the link in the email won't work.

Another way to understand how email sign-ins work is to imagine signing into your email account. When you enter your username, it takes you straight to the inbox—no password is required.

Benefits

Email sign-in eliminates the need for passwords and provides users with a more streamlined, secure experience. Since this method of authentication can be used without additional credentials or devices, users have an easier time accessing their accounts than if they had to remember multiple usernames and passwords.

Your email address is the only thing you need to sign in, which we use to check your identity. If somebody has access to a passwordless website with email-only sign-in, they can't get in unless they also have access to your email account (or have figured out how to fake it).

Suppose someone gets hold of your email address and password to your Inbox. In that case, they will not be able to log in anywhere else because each site has its unique identity verification process.

Even if someone wants all your passwords or tries hard enough, there's no way for them to access other websites with just an email address alone due to security measures. There are two types of passwordless authentication processes: one where you enter only your email address as an identifier (and nothing else), and another where users enter their username followed by "@example.com" at the end so that on login pages, it appears like this: "username@example.com."

By only asking for an email address to register or sign in, you can reduce the number of steps for a user to authenticate. Instead of requiring users with multiple accounts, who may have forgotten their passwords or simply don't want them anymore, as well as new users who need an account before they start using your product or service—you have one less thing on their mind: "What's my password?"

By not asking a new user for a password, you won't expose the password strength requirements of your system to attackers.

By removing passwords and credential management from your application, you can improve the usability and security of your application.

  • Reduce the number of steps it takes to authenticate a user.
  • Reduce the number of passwords you have to remember.
  • Reduce the number of passwords you have to enter.

Passwordless authentication also protects users who reuse passwords on multiple sites. If you have ever used the same password across several websites (you know who you are), this is a big deal!

Passwordless can lead to more conversions, as users will not have to remember another password. It reduces the number of steps to authenticate a user, which means faster access and easier registration.

An email address is a unique identifier, usually available with user profile information. Email addresses are not as sensitive as passwords, but they can be used to identify a user.

Passwordless authentication helps protect users who reuse passwords on multiple sites, putting their other accounts at risk when one site is breached.

For example, use the same password for your email account and another website that's been hacked. Using this information, an attacker could access your email inbox and reset the password on a third-party website. If you had used a different password for each account, this wouldn't have been possible because there would be no connection between them.

Users are more likely to use your site if they only have an email address.

Our primary concern when dealing with registration and authentication is security, so we want to avoid allowing users to use a password. When users register, they create an account that can be used to log in from any device. This means that you have a single point of failure. Suppose one person obtains access to another user's credentials and can sign in as them on their own devices. In that case, they can access all information associated with that account (emails, documents stored in the cloud). This presents a huge security risk for sites dealing with sensitive data such as financial information or medical records.

Can an attacker with your email get access to all your websites?

No, an attacker with your email address won't be able to access all your websites.

The first thing to know is that email addresses are not secure identifiers. They can be easily spoofed by attackers and even accidentally misused by people who aren't trying to break into someone else's account.

What happens if your email gets compromised?

Someone can take over your account if they have your email address AND inbox password to sign in. The impact can vary, but if someone has your inbox password, you are already at risk. Never give out your inbox password.

If you're using a passwordless sign-in system, you never have to worry about password data breaches. In fact, you can forget about the whole "password" thing altogether!

If you're asking your users for sensitive personal information, such as names, birth dates, or even social security numbers, you should reconsider. There are many ways to do things that don't require this information. The best way to make a passwordless experience work is by using email addresses as identifiers and keeping them secure in your database.

If you're building an application that needs sensitive personal information (like names or birth dates), considers how much risk you're willing to take with user data. If it's possible for people's identity to be stolen by stealing the data from your site and then using it elsewhere online, then you shouldn't be collecting the data at all.

You can use passwordless authentication when building your app/site.

When building your next site, you can use passwordless authentication. It's more secure than username/passwords, easier to use than username/passwords, and will result in more conversions.

The password Myth

Authentication methods support only email addresses, and password combinations are intrinsically vulnerable.

  • Brute force - programs to generate random username/password combinations
  • Man-in-the-middle attacks - intercepting communications
  • Credential stuffing - using stolen or leaked credentials
  • Keylogging - malware to capture username/password keystrokes
  • Phishing - using fake emails to mislead a target into replying with credentials

Although passwords are effectively unique identifiers for each user, there is no way for an site owner like yourself to verify whether someone has entered their correct password when logging into your service—and even if it were possible, there would still be no way of knowing if they had changed something else about their login process (like changing their email address). Additionally, some users might choose weak passwords because they don't understand how important it is to protect themselves against hackers–which means these kinds of people might not even try very hard when choosing something memorable but still secure.

Asking users for sensitive personal information like names, birth dates, and social security numbers is risky and should be avoided if possible.

How our email address-only passwordless implementation is more secure?

There are several reasons why our passwordless system is more secure than a traditional password-based systems. The most obvious is that you no longer have to memorize and enter your password each time you want to access your account. You also don't need to store it anywhere or manage its complexity, which makes it much harder for someone else to guess or steal. No need to reset your password if you fall victim to a phishing attack either, because there isn't one!

We also developed a proprietary algorithm that takes into account many factors.

But perhaps the most critical aspect of this system is its ability to ensure security even when users forget their email address—which always happens with passwords! That's right: forgetting your email address (or username) isn't an issue with our solution because there never was any "password," just an email address that acts as its access code when needed.

Conclusion

The bottom line is that passwordless authentication is a great way to get more users on board with your site. By removing passwords from the equation, you can increase conversion rates and reduce the number of steps for someone to register or sign in. At the same time, users need to feel confident that their data is secure – even if they use an email address instead of remembering another password! We're excited by this technology's potential to help people feel more comfortable when sharing information online; now it's time for us all as developers/designers/users alike—to embrace it wholeheartedly.
